# Prompt

Why is the sky blue?

# Response

The sky appears blue primarily due to a phenomenon called Rayleigh scattering. Here's a step-by-step explanation:

1. **Sunlight Composition**: Sunlight, or more generally, white light from the sun, contains all colors of the visible spectrum (red, orange, yellow, green, blue, indigo, and violet).

2. **Interaction with Atmosphere**: When sunlight enters Earth's atmosphere, it collides with molecules in the air and tiny particles such as dust and water droplets.

3. **Scattering of Light**: The scattering effect is more pronounced for shorter wavelengths (blue and violet) than for longer wavelengths (red, orange, yellow). This is because the amount of scattering is inversely proportional to the fourth power of the wavelength (\(I \propto \frac{1}{\lambda^4}\)), a relationship described by Rayleigh's law.

4. **Wavelength Comparison**: Blue light has a shorter wavelength (approximately 475 nm) compared to red light (approximately 650 nm). Consequently, blue light is scattered much more strongly than red or other longer wavelengths.

5. **Perception from Earth**: When we look up at the sky on a clear day, our eyes receive the widely scattered blue light from all directions in the atmosphere. The violet light, which has an even shorter wavelength, is scattered even more but is less visible to the human eye and much of it is absorbed by the upper atmosphere. Thus, the cumulative effect of scattered blue light makes the sky appear predominantly blue.

6. **Sunrise and Sunset Colors**: During sunrise and sunset, sunlight travels through a greater thickness of the atmosphere. This longer path results in more scattering of shorter wavelengths (blue) out of our line of sight, allowing the longer wavelengths (red, orange, yellow) to dominate the visible spectrum, giving rise to the characteristic reds and oranges of sunrises and sunsets.

In summary, the blue color of the sky is a result of Rayleigh scattering, where shorter wavelengths of light (blue) are scattered more efficiently by the molecules in Earth's atmosphere than longer wavelengths (red), leading to the predominant blue hue we observe during daylight.
